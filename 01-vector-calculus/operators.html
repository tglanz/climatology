1  Differential Operators

Let \(f : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}\) be a scalar field and \(\mathbf{F} : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3\) a vector field with components \(\mathbf{F} = (F_x, F_y, F_z)\).

1.1 Gradient

Definition (scalar field). The gradient of \(f : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}\) is the vector field

\[\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right).\]

Intuition. \(\nabla f(\mathbf{p})\) points in the direction of steepest increase of \(f\) at \(\mathbf{p}\). Its magnitude is the rate of change in that direction. Level sets of \(f\) are everywhere perpendicular to \(\nabla f\).

Example. Let \(f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2\). Then \(\nabla f = (2x, 2y)\). At \(\mathbf{p} = (1, 1)\): \(\nabla f = (2, 2)\), pointing radially outward with magnitude \(2\sqrt{2}\).

Definition (vector field). The gradient of \(\mathbf{F} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) is the \(m \times n\) tensor of partial derivatives

\[(\nabla \mathbf{F})_{ij} = \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial x_j}.\]

This is a second-order tensor (matrix). It maps a direction \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\) to the directional derivative \(D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} \mathbf{F} = (\nabla \mathbf{F})\,\hat{\mathbf{u}}\), generalizing the scalar case.

Example. Let \(\mathbf{F}(x, y) = (x^2 + y,\; xy)\). Then

\[\nabla \mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} 2x & 1 \\ y & x \end{pmatrix}.\]

At \(\mathbf{p} = (1, 2)\): \(\nabla \mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\).

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 9.7 (scalar gradient and directional derivative); tensor gradient of vector fields is treated more fully in advanced calculus texts.

1.2 Jacobian

Definition. For \(\mathbf{F} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\), the Jacobian matrix \(J_\mathbf{F}\) is identical to \(\nabla \mathbf{F}\):

\[J_\mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_n} \end{pmatrix}.\]

The Jacobian is the best linear approximation to \(\mathbf{F}\) at a point: \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{h}) \approx \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{p}) + J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{p})\,\mathbf{h}\).

Jacobian determinant. For square \(\mathbf{F} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n\), \(\det(J_\mathbf{F})\) measures local volume scaling under \(\mathbf{F}\). It appears in the change-of-variables formula:

\[\int_{\mathbf{F}(V)} g(\mathbf{y})\, d\mathbf{y} = \int_V g(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}))\,|\det J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})|\, d\mathbf{x}.\]

Example. The map to spherical coordinates \(\mathbf{F}(r, \theta, \phi) = (r\sin\theta\cos\phi,\; r\sin\theta\sin\phi,\; r\cos\theta)\) has

\[\det(J_\mathbf{F}) = r^2 \sin\theta,\]

giving the volume element \(dV = r^2\sin\theta\, dr\, d\theta\, d\phi\).

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 9.6 (calculus review, change of variables) and Sec. 10.7 (Jacobian in the context of triple integrals).

1.3 Directional Derivative

Definition. The directional derivative of \(f\) at \(\mathbf{p}\) along unit vector \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\) is

\[D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{p}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{p}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}.\]

This is the rate of change of \(f\) along \(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\). The gradient is the unique direction that maximizes \(D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f\), with maximum value \(|\nabla f(\mathbf{p})|\).

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 9.7.

1.4 Divergence

Definition. The divergence of \(\mathbf{F}\) is the scalar field

\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z}.\]

Intuition. \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{p})\) measures the net outward flux of \(\mathbf{F}\) per unit volume at \(\mathbf{p}\). Positive divergence indicates a local source; negative indicates a sink; zero indicates incompressibility.

Example. Let \(\mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = (x, y, z)\) (radial expansion). Then

\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3.\]

Uniform positive divergence everywhere: every point is a source.

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 9.8.

1.5 Curl

Definition. The curl of \(\mathbf{F}\) is the vector field

\[\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{\mathbf{x}} & \hat{\mathbf{y}} & \hat{\mathbf{z}} \\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \\ F_x & F_y & F_z \end{vmatrix} = \left(\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z},\; \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y}\right).\]

Intuition. \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{p})\) measures the infinitesimal rotation of \(\mathbf{F}\) at \(\mathbf{p}\). Its direction is the axis of rotation (right-hand rule); its magnitude is twice the angular velocity. A field with \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) everywhere is called irrotational.

Example. Let \(\mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = (-y, x, 0)\), solid-body rotation in the \(xy\)-plane. Then

\[\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left(0 - 0,\; 0 - 0,\; 1 - (-1)\right) = (0, 0, 2).\]

The rotation axis is \(+\hat{\mathbf{z}}\) with angular velocity \(1\).

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 9.9.

1.6 Laplacian

Definition. The Laplacian of \(f\) is

\[\nabla^2 f = \nabla \cdot (\nabla f) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z^2}.\]

For a vector field, the Laplacian acts component-wise: \(\nabla^2 \mathbf{F} = (\nabla^2 F_x, \nabla^2 F_y, \nabla^2 F_z)\).

Intuition. \(\nabla^2 f(\mathbf{p})\) is proportional to the difference between \(f(\mathbf{p})\) and the average of \(f\) over an infinitesimal sphere centered at \(\mathbf{p}\). It governs diffusion, electrostatics, and fluid flow. The equation \(\nabla^2 f = 0\) (Laplace equation) characterizes fields with no local sources or sinks.

Example. Let \(f(x, y) = x^2 - y^2\). Then \(\nabla^2 f = 2 - 2 = 0\): this function satisfies the Laplace equation.

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 9.8 (Laplacian as div of grad); @Evans2010pde, Ch. 2 (Laplace equation and its properties).
