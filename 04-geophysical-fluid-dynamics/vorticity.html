12  Vorticity

12.1 Definitions

Let \(\vec{v}(\vec{x}, t)\) be the velocity field. The vorticity is:

\[ \vec{\omega} = \nabla \times \vec{v} \]

Vorticity measures local rotation of fluid elements. A flow with \(\vec{\omega} = 0\) everywhere is irrotational.

The circulation around a closed curve \(C\) is:

\[ \Gamma = \oint_C \vec{v} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S \vec{\omega} \cdot d\vec{S} \]

where \(S\) is any surface bounded by \(C\) (by Stokes’ theorem). Circulation is the integrated vorticity through the surface, or equivalently the net tangential velocity around the loop.

12.2 Vorticity Equation

Take the curl of the momentum equation for an inviscid barotropic fluid (\(p = p(\rho)\)):

\[ \frac{\partial \vec{\omega}}{\partial t} + (\vec{v} \cdot \nabla)\vec{\omega} = (\vec{\omega} \cdot \nabla)\vec{v} - \vec{\omega}(\nabla \cdot \vec{v}) \]

The left-hand side is advection of vorticity. On the right:

  • \((\vec{\omega} \cdot \nabla)\vec{v}\) is vortex stretching and tilting. Velocity gradients along the vorticity direction amplify or reorient vortex tubes.
  • \(-\vec{\omega}(\nabla \cdot \vec{v})\) accounts for divergence: expanding fluid dilutes vorticity.

For incompressible flow (\(\nabla \cdot \vec{v} = 0\)), the second term vanishes.

A baroclinic term \(\frac{1}{\rho^2} \nabla \rho \times \nabla p\) appears when density is not a function of pressure alone. It generates vorticity wherever isosurfaces of \(\rho\) and \(p\) are misaligned.

12.3 Kelvin’s Circulation Theorem

For an inviscid, barotropic fluid with only conservative body forces, the circulation around a material curve \(C(t)\) (a curve moving with the fluid) is conserved:

\[ \frac{D\Gamma}{Dt} = 0 \]

Vortex lines are frozen into the fluid: they stretch and deform with it but cannot be created or destroyed. This breaks down when viscosity, baroclinicity, or non-conservative forces are present.

12.4 Vorticity Equation in a Rotating Frame

In a frame rotating with angular velocity \(\vec{\Omega}\), define the absolute vorticity \(\vec{\omega}_a = \vec{\omega} + 2\vec{\Omega}\). The vorticity equation becomes:

\[ \frac{D\vec{\omega}_a}{Dt} = (\vec{\omega}_a \cdot \nabla)\vec{v} - \vec{\omega}_a(\nabla \cdot \vec{v}) \]

The planetary vorticity \(2\vec{\Omega}\) enters on equal footing with the relative vorticity \(\vec{\omega}\). For large-scale geophysical flows, \(|2\vec{\Omega}| \gg |\vec{\omega}|\), so planetary rotation dominates the vorticity budget.

Restricting to the vertical component for shallow horizontal flow, with \(f = 2\Omega \sin\varphi\):

\[ \frac{D}{Dt}(\zeta + f) = -(\zeta + f)\nabla_h \cdot \vec{v}_h \]

where \(\zeta = \hat{k} \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{v})\) is the vertical component of relative vorticity and \(\nabla_h \cdot \vec{v}_h\) is the horizontal divergence.

12.5 Circulation Theorem and the \(\beta\)-effect

Kelvin’s theorem applied to the rotating Earth conserves absolute circulation. A fluid column moving poleward encounters increasing \(f\), so \(\zeta\) must decrease to compensate. This coupling between meridional motion and relative vorticity is the \(\beta\)-effect and is the restoring mechanism for Rossby waves.

12.6 Barotropic Vorticity Equation

For a two-dimensional, incompressible, inviscid flow on a \(\beta\)-plane, horizontal divergence vanishes (\(\nabla_h \cdot \vec{v}_h = 0\)). The vorticity equation reduces to:

\[ \frac{\partial \zeta}{\partial t} + J(\psi, \zeta) + \beta \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} = 0 \]

where \(\psi\) is the streamfunction defined by \(u = -\partial \psi / \partial y\), \(v = \partial \psi / \partial x\), and \(J(\psi, \zeta) = \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}\frac{\partial \zeta}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial y}\frac{\partial \zeta}{\partial x}\) is the Jacobian. Since \(\zeta = \nabla^2 \psi\), this is a single prognostic equation for \(\psi\).

This is the Barotropic Vorticity Equation (BVE) - the PDE that the GSNO will be trained to solve.
