6  Fourier Transform

6.1 Definition

The Fourier transform extends Fourier series to functions on \(\mathbb{R}\), replacing the discrete sum over frequencies with a continuous integral.

Definition. For \(f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})\), the Fourier transform is

\[\hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, e^{-2\pi i \xi x}\, dx.\]

The inverse Fourier transform recovers \(f\) from \(\hat{f}\):

\[f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi)\, e^{2\pi i \xi x}\, d\xi.\]

\(\hat{f}(\xi)\) is the amplitude of the frequency component \(\xi\) in \(f\). The transform is well-defined and invertible for \(f \in L^2(\mathbb{R})\) (Plancherel theorem).

Alternative convention. With angular frequency \(\omega = 2\pi\xi\):

\[\hat{f}(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\, e^{-i\omega x}\, dx, \qquad f(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\omega)\, e^{i\omega x}\, d\omega.\]

Both conventions are standard; the choice affects normalization factors.

6.2 Key Properties

Let \(\mathcal{F}[f] = \hat{f}\).

Linearity: \(\mathcal{F}[\alpha f + \beta g] = \alpha\hat{f} + \beta\hat{g}\).

Shift: \(\mathcal{F}[f(x - a)](\xi) = e^{-2\pi i \xi a}\hat{f}(\xi)\). Shifting in space is multiplication by a phase in frequency.

Derivative: \(\mathcal{F}[f^{(n)}](\xi) = (2\pi i \xi)^n \hat{f}(\xi)\). Differentiation in space is multiplication by \(i\xi\) in frequency. This is the key property that converts differential equations into algebraic equations.

Convolution theorem: For \((f * g)(x) = \int f(y)\, g(x-y)\, dy\):

\[\mathcal{F}[f * g] = \hat{f} \cdot \hat{g}.\]

Convolution in the spatial domain is pointwise multiplication in the frequency domain.

Parseval / Plancherel:

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x)|^2\, dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2\, d\xi.\]

6.3 Differential Equations via Fourier Transform

The derivative property makes the Fourier transform a natural tool for solving linear PDEs with constant coefficients. Applying \(\mathcal{F}\) converts \(\partial_x \to 2\pi i\xi\), turning a PDE into an algebraic equation in frequency space.

Example. Solve \(f'(x) + af(x) = g(x)\) on \(\mathbb{R}\). Taking the Fourier transform:

\[(2\pi i\xi + a)\hat{f}(\xi) = \hat{g}(\xi) \implies \hat{f}(\xi) = \frac{\hat{g}(\xi)}{2\pi i\xi + a}.\]

Inverting gives \(f = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[\hat{g}/(2\pi i\xi + a)]\). This solution is a convolution \(f = g * h\) where \(h = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[1/(2\pi i\xi + a)]\) is the impulse response - the Green’s function of the operator \(\partial_x + a\).

6.4 Discrete Fourier Transform

For a finite sequence \(x_0, \ldots, x_{N-1}\), the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is

\[X_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n\, e^{-2\pi i kn/N}, \qquad k = 0, \ldots, N-1.\]

The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) computes the DFT in \(O(N\log N)\) rather than \(O(N^2)\). The convolution theorem holds exactly for the DFT, making the FFT the computational backbone of spectral methods and the Fourier Neural Operator.
