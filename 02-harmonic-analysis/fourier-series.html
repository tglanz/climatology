7  Fourier Series

The Fourier series is the concrete realization of the abstract ONB expansion from the function spaces section, applied to \(L^2([-\pi,\pi])\). The basis is the family of complex exponentials \(e^{inx}\), which are the eigenfunctions of \(-d^2/dx^2\) with periodic boundary conditions - a Sturm-Liouville problem on \([-\pi,\pi]\).

7.1 Definition and Coefficients

The functions \(\phi_n(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{inx}\), \(n \in \mathbb{Z}\), form an orthonormal basis for \(L^2([-\pi,\pi])\) under the inner product \(\langle f,g\rangle = \int_{-\pi}^\pi f\bar{g}\, dx\).

Definition. The Fourier series of \(f \in L^2([-\pi, \pi])\) is

\[f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n\, e^{inx}, \qquad c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\, e^{-inx}\, dx.\]

The \(c_n = \langle f, \phi_n\rangle\) are the generalized Fourier coefficients from the abstract theory. The series converges in \(L^2\); Parseval’s identity gives \(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi |f|^2\,dx = \sum_n |c_n|^2\).

Real form. For real \(f\), writing \(e^{inx} = \cos(nx) + i\sin(nx)\):

\[f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigl(a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx)\bigr),\]

where \(a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos(nx)\, dx\) and \(b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin(nx)\, dx\).

Example. Let \(f(x) = x\) on \([-\pi, \pi]\). Then \(a_n = 0\) (odd function) and \(b_n = \frac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\):

\[x = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nx) = 2\left(\sin x - \frac{\sin 2x}{2} + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} - \cdots\right).\]

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Secs. 11.1-11.2; @BruntonKutz2022, Sec. 2.1.

7.2 Convergence

For \(f \in L^2\), the Fourier series converges in mean square but need not converge pointwise everywhere. Key results:

  • If \(f\) is piecewise smooth, the series converges to \(f(x)\) at continuity points and to \(\frac{1}{2}[f(x^+) + f(x^-)]\) at jump discontinuities (Gibbs phenomenon near jumps).
  • If \(f \in C^k\), then \(|c_n| = O(n^{-k})\): smoother functions have faster-decaying coefficients.

Further reading: @Kreyszig2011aem, Sec. 11.1 (convergence discussion).

7.3 Worked Example: Square Wave

Let \(f\) be the \(2\pi\)-periodic square wave:

\[f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & 0 < x < \pi \\ -1 & -\pi < x < 0 \end{cases}.\]

Since \(f\) is odd, all cosine coefficients vanish: \(a_n = 0\). For the sine coefficients:

\[b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin(nx)\, dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\sin(nx)\, dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\left[-\frac{\cos(nx)}{n}\right]_0^{\pi} = \frac{2}{n\pi}(1 - \cos(n\pi)).\]

Since \(\cos(n\pi) = (-1)^n\):

\[b_n = \frac{2}{n\pi}(1 - (-1)^n) = \begin{cases} 4/(n\pi) & n \text{ odd} \\ 0 & n \text{ even} \end{cases}.\]

The Fourier series is

\[f(x) = \frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{\sin((2k+1)x)}{2k+1} = \frac{4}{\pi}\left(\sin x + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} + \frac{\sin 5x}{5} + \cdots\right).\]

The coefficients decay as \(O(1/n)\), consistent with \(f \in C^0\) but \(f \notin C^1\) (jump discontinuities). Near the jumps, the partial sums overshoot by approximately \(18\%\) regardless of how many terms are included (Gibbs phenomenon).
